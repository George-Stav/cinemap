# DESIGN TENETS

1. The map is the canvas, not the content. Every interface element floats on top of a map-like surface. UI should feel like it's hovering above the world, not replacing it. Panels, buttons, and overlays use semi-transparent white with backdrop blur so the map remains visible and present at all times.

2. Posters over pins. Visual identity of a place is shown through the content inside it, not generic markers. Whatever represents the location (a movie poster, a dish, a product) becomes the marker itself. This principle makes every CineSpot product immediately scannable — you see what before you read where.

3. Gold is a supporting actor, not the star. The cinema gold (#EF9F27) appears sparingly — on the user's location, active states, primary CTAs, and accent details. It signals "cinema" without overwhelming the content it frames. The real color comes from the posters, films, and locations themselves.

4. Never leave the map. Users should never feel they've navigated away from where they are. Details slide up from below, overlays dim what's not relevant, panels sit on top of the map. Full-screen page changes break the sense of place and should be avoided.

5. Two modes, one flow. Every product operates in one of two states at any moment: Explore mode (everything visible, wide-open browsing) or Focus mode (one subject highlighted, everything else dimmed). Transitions between them should feel like a spotlight turning on — not a page reload.

6. Information density scales with intent. A glance shows the essential (poster, name, next time). A tap reveals structure (showtimes, screens, prices). A second tap delivers action (book, navigate, save). Never show more than the user needs for their current decision.

7. Time is the primary filter. Temporal relevance drives what's shown first. Upcoming beats past, today beats tomorrow, nearby-now beats far-later. Dim or hide what's no longer actionable; lead with what's happening next.

8. Typography carries the brand, not decoration. One distinctive sans-serif (DM Sans), two weights (regular and bold), tight letter-spacing on headings, generous line-height on body. No ornaments, no illustrations, no icon-heavy interfaces. The content does the work.
